A common practice during the development of software systems is the use of frameworks and libraries [Kim et al. 2006], facilitating reuse through Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) [Konstantopoulos et al. 2009], and increasing the productivity of development teams [Moser and Nierstrasz 1996]. However, frameworks / libraries are constantly evolving, hence their APIs are subject to change. That way, as APIs evolve, it is desirable that your client systems be upgraded to take advantage of the updates.

During the evolution of an API, methods, classes or fields can be modified, renamed or removed. In order to facilitate the transition process for customers to adopt updates of these API elements, developers can make use of the depreciation mechanism. Through depreciation, you also need to maintain compatibility between the API and your clients. However, some best practices should be adopted to help the developers of client systems transition to a new version of the used APIs. One such good practice is to depreciate the APIs with clear messages indicating how to treat the depreciated element or provide a possible indication of how to replace it. In order to facilitate the depreciation process, many programming languages offer mechanisms to deprecate elements of APIs with messages, i.e. Java makes use of the @deprecated Javadoc tag. However, there are studies that point out that APIs are often deprecated with obscure messages, or even without messages, making it difficult to update their clients [Robbes et al. 2012]. However, these studies do not use visualization techniques that can aid in obtaining relevant information to the study of depreciation phenomena. In this context, this site features views on API Depreciation and Replace Messages.
